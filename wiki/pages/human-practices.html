Human Practices

Connecting Metlock to real stakeholders and real constraints.

Human Practices is where Metlock is tested against the world beyond the bench. The project is not only about whether engineered cells can interact with zinc, but also whether that work fits real environmental needs, industrial workflows, safety expectations, and community concerns.

This page organizes the social, environmental, and stakeholder-facing side of the project into one clear place so the design can stay grounded in practical use, responsible development, and long-term implementation thinking.

Human Practices Overview

Why this project needs more than a lab-only story.

Zinc recovery sits at the intersection of resource demand, environmental damage, industrial practicality, and public trust. That means Metlock has to be evaluated not only for scientific promise, but also for where it might fit, what risks it could create, and which users or communities would actually benefit from it.

Human Practices gives the project a framework for asking those questions early. It helps the team connect laboratory design choices to stakeholder priorities, implementation realities, and responsible deployment constraints. This page is meant to support long-form reflection and stakeholder analysis without turning the material into a visually fragmented dashboard.

Human Practices focus The goal is to show how external context shapes the project, not to treat outreach and responsibility as separate from design.
Stakeholder Mapping

Who shapes the direction of Metlock.

Relevant stakeholders include mining and minerals experts, environmental scientists and engineers, communities affected by metal pollution, and future researchers who may reuse or adapt the platform. Each group asks different questions about feasibility, safety, communication, and practical value.

A strong stakeholder analysis does not only list audiences. It should explain what each group cares about, what information they need before trusting the project, and how their feedback changes priorities within the design process.

Community / Industry / Environmental Context

The problem surrounding the science.

The demand for critical minerals continues to grow, but conventional extraction and waste handling can impose major ecological and social costs. Zinc recovery and metal-rich wastewater contexts therefore create an important test case for whether synthetic biology can add selectivity or flexibility without simply moving the burden elsewhere.

For Metlock, that means thinking about dilute zinc streams, industrial polishing steps, contaminated environments, and what a realistic interface between biology and existing recovery infrastructure might look like. This section is designed to hold the longer contextual narrative that supports why the project matters outside the lab.

How Feedback Shapes Design

Turning outside input into design decisions.

Stakeholder conversations become useful when they clearly change what the team builds, tests, or prioritizes. For Metlock, that might mean refining target use cases, narrowing performance goals, clarifying safety expectations, or making assay outputs more meaningful to people outside the immediate research team.

As this page grows, it can document which questions came from consultations, which concerns reshaped the project, and which practical constraints helped the team decide where the platform should be realistic first.

Feedback logic Human Practices is strongest when outside input can be tied directly to a design choice, a communication change, or a shift in implementation strategy.
Safety, Ethics, and Implementation Considerations

Questions the project has to answer responsibly.

This section is intended for deeper writing on containment, responsible deployment, environmental benefit, and how the project should be framed if it ever moves beyond tightly controlled lab settings. It should also be the place where the team reflects on what kinds of claims are justified now and which ones should remain exploratory.

Implementation discussion can also live here: what kinds of treatment systems make sense first, what conditions would make a biological approach attractive, and what evidence would be needed before the platform could be taken seriously in industrial or environmental settings.
