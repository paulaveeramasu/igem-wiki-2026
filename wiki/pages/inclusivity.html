Inclusivity

Polluted wastewater goes beyond environmental hazards. It’s a human rights issue affecting public health, local economies, and justice. Certain countries are marginalized, and bear the harmful effects of wastewater pollution, a problem that needs to be addressed [2].

Most importantly, polluted wastewater is damaging to human health and well-being. It carries pathogens, heavy metals, prescriptions, and chemicals unfiltered by common treatment systems. Reports of medieval diseases are common among these communities, as well as reports of estrogen affecting the sex of fish. Limited sanitation and poor infrastructure contribute to poorly managed wastewater [5]. Immunocompromised individuals are most at risk for these diseases, especially in communities with a lack of healthcare [6].

Polluted wastewater usually affects communities struggling with environmental justice. Industries and landfills locate their facilities near low-income neighborhoods and practice unsafe sanitation techniques for a reduced fee, increasing the divide that political injustice creates. They are left to live with foul water, lowered biodiversity, and depleted crops. Pollution is rooted in injustice through a lack of political decision-making power and lobbying [1].

Beyond health and environment, polluted wastewater damages maintenance. Sewage systems degrade with the constant passage of chemicals and are often old and unkempt. This leads to sewage overflows, flooding, and raw wastewater backup. Preventive maintenance is replaced with expensive emergency responses [5].

Inequality is rooted in political divides and economic struggle. Health, corrosion, and lack of funds are the main causes of polluted wastewater [6]. Fixing these problems at their core will prove to be more sustainable and affordable for the public in the future. Focusing on improving water quality is essential to the safety and longevity of these communities [3].
